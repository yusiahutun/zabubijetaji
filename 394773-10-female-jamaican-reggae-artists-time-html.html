10 best female Jamaican Reggae artists of all time

February 19, 2024
blog

Reggae music has produced many big names over the years, but unfortunately, most of them are male. The mention of renowned reggae musicians of all-time springs to mind names such as Bob Marley, Burning Spear, and Dennis Brown. Men might dominate the industry, but this is not to say that there are no women who conquered reggae. The ten best female Jamaican reggae artists provide a glimmer of hope for women that the future is theirs for the taking.

Even in reggae music, there is an embodiment of the many challenges women undergo ranging from sexism to the burden of motherhood. The talent has always been there but maintaining a high level of performance and consistency remains problematic. This stands in the way of Jamaican female singers achieving their potential.

Female Jamaican Reggae artists

Reggae has long been known as one of the greatest ways of connecting people from different cultural backgrounds. The artists use this music to advocate for love and peace among all. Here is a list of some of the best female Jamaican reggae artists of all time.

1. Marcia Griffiths

Marcia Griffith is one of Jamaica's most successful female reggae artist. She has been producing hit music for more than five decades after launching her solo career in the 1960s. She did not only achieve success in her home country but internationally as well.

Her collaboration with singer Bob Andy in the remake of Nina Simone's Young Gifted and Black and their follow up Pied Piper all topped UK's BBC charts. The star has pioneered the worldwide Electric Slide dance craze that spawned from her 1980's single Electric Boogie.

She developed a reputation for her strong and yet smooth love songs that were often accompanied by captivating live performances. Her albums include:

  • Timeless (2019)
  • Marcia Griffiths & Friends (2012)
  • Melody Life (2007)
  • Shining Time (2005)

2. Phyllis Dillon

There cannot be mention of the best female roots reggae artists without Dillon's name. She passed away on April 15, 2004, aged 59. The singer's music still lives on cementing her place as one of the greats of reggae music.

After being inspired by the likes of Connie Francis and Dionne Warwick, Dillon started singing in talent contests, and that is how Duke Reid discovered her. Don't Stay Away (1966) is the first record she recorded for Duke.

Most of her subsequent recordings were covers of popular American songs, but her ability was never in question. In 1972, she released the album One Life to Live and then followed it up with compilations such as:

  • Love Is All I Had (1994)
  • Rhino
  • One Life to Live (2000)
  • Rhino and Midnight Confessions: Classic Rocksteady and Reggae (2000)
  • Westside

3. Judy Mowatt

Judy Mowatt has been a singer since 1967 to date. At one point, she was a member of the I Three trio that served as backing vocalists for Bob Marley & The Wailers. Many critics have labelled her Black Woman album as the greatest reggae album by a female artist.

It was the first reggae album to be recorded by a woman who doubled as a producer. Mowatt was the first female singer to be nominated for a Grammy Award in the category of Reggae music in 1985.

Judy used to identify herself with the Rastafari movement, but after converting to Christianity, she started singing gospel music. Some of her albums include:

  • Sing Our Song (2003)
  • Something Old
  • Something New (2002)
  • Rock Me (1993)
  • Working Wonders (1985)

4. Millie Small

After a long, illustrious career, Millie Small died on May 5, 2020. She was a Jamaican singer and songwriter famous from her 1964 My Boy Lollipop which peaked at number two both in the UK Singles Chart and the US Billboard Hot 100.

Millie was the Caribbean's first international recording star and one of the most successful female performers. Her career began when she won the Vere Johns Opportunity Hour talent contest aged 12. She then relocated to Kingston to launch her singing career.

She paired up with Owen Gray, and the duo made several records. Her albums include:

  • My Boy Lollipop (1964)
  • Time Will Tell (1970/2004)
  • My Boy Lollipop (1994)

5. Diana King

Diana King performs a mixture of reggae-fusion and dancehall. The singer's 1995 hit single Shy Guy gave her fame. In 1994, she appeared in The Notorious BIG's song Respect.

Her 1995 single, Shy Guy took ten minutes to write, but it became a hit reaching number 13 on the Billboard Hot 100 and became certified gold by the RIAA in the US. She has more than five studio albums to her name including:

  • Tougher Than Love (1995)
  • Think Like a Girl (1997)
  • Respect (2002)
  • Warrior Gurl (2010)

6. J.C. Lodge

J.C. Lodge was born to a Jamaican father and British mother in England. The reggae singer is also an actress and fine artist. Her breakthrough came with the 1982 hit song Someone Loves You Honey which became the best-selling single of 1982 in the Netherlands.

Her musical career began with her relationship with Errol O'Meally, who was a songwriter. O'Meally fell in love with her voice and used it to present some of his compositions to Joe Gibbs' Recording Studio. She was well-received leading to a career of over 13 albums including:

  • Revealed (1985)
  • I Believe in You (1987)
  • Selfish Lover (1990)
  • To the Max (1993)
  • Let Love Inside (2001)
  • This is Crucial Reggae (2004)

7. Janet Kay

Janet Kay occupies a special place in reggae music. She was born in Willesden, North West London, England. The singer used to sing at a rehearsal studio, and that is how she was discovered by Tony Robinson, a keyboardist from Aswad.

Robinson recommended Kay to Alton Ellis who comes from Jamaica but had relocated to London. In 1978, Kay recorded I Do Love You, and That's What Friends Are For. She rocked the world of reggae with her 1979 hit Silly Games which topped both the UK and European charts.

She entered the Music Guinness Book of Records as the first British born black female to have a reggae song top the British charts. Her albums include:

  • Capricorn Woman (1982)
  • Sweet Surrender (1989)
  • Love You Always (1993)
  • Idol Kay (2012)

8. Sandra Cross

Sandra Cross was born to Jamaican parents but raised in London, England. She has released about 12 albums in the last two decades. She is one of the leading forces of rock music in England.

Her hit single I Adore You which she released at the age of 14 reached number 1 in the UK reggae charts. Four of her other singles also claimed the number 1 spot. She also joined the all-girl band called the Wild Bunch whose only album reached the Top 20 chart in 1984.

Her singles such as Country Living, You're Lying, and many others topped the British Charts. She is a six-time winner of the British Reggae Awards for the Best Female Singer.

9. Grace Jones

Grace Beverly Jones is a Jamaican singer, model, songwriter, actress and record producer. She was an influential figure in the cross-dressing movement and became an inspiration for artists such as Lady Gaga, Rihanna, and Solange. Billboard magazine ranked her as the 40th greatest dance club artist of all time.

Jones began her career in 1977 when Island Records signed her. Her style of music was a blend of reggae, post-punk, funk and pop music. She is known for albums such as:

  • Warm Leatherette (1980)
  • Slave to the Rhythm (1985)
  • Nightclubbing (1981)

10. Patra

Dorothy Smith, popularly known as Patra is a Jamaican reggae and dancehall singer. She began her career as a dancehall deejay in the 1980s before transitioning to become one of the best female reggae singers. She made a name for herself in the US when Shabba Ranks featured her in the song Family Affair.

In 1995, she released the single Pull Up to the Bumper, a remake of the Grace Jones song by the same title. Her other albums include:

  • Scent of Attraction (1995)
  • The Great Escape (2003)
  • Where I've Been (2005)
