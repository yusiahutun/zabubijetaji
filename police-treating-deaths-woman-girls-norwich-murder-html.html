Norwich house bloodbath was triple-murder suicide, police believe: Post-mortems reveal girls aged 8

March 12, 2024
blog

Detectives believe the deaths of a man, woman and two girls were a triple-murder-suicide after post-mortem examinations confirmed the four died of stab wounds.

The force said the deaths of Kanticha Sukpengpanao, 36, Jasmin Kuczynska, 12 and Natasha Kuczynska, eight, are being treated as murder.

The death of Bartlomiej Kuczynski, 45, who was found with what is believed to be a self-inflicted stab wound to his neck, is not being treated as suspicious.

Officers are not looking for anyone else in connection with the killings after their bodies were discovered by police at a house in Allan Bedford Crescent, Costessey, following a call from a member of the public at 7am on Friday.

It later emerged that Kuczynski had called the police just one hour earlier saying he was concerned about his 'mental state' â€“ only to be told by officers to seek medical help himself, according to police watchdog the IOPC.

Jasmin, 12, and her sister Natasha, nine, were described as 'beautiful girls' by neighbours

The girl's aunt Kanticha Sukpengpanao - also known as Kanticha Noon - was also found dead in the property

The death of Bartlomiej Kuczynski , 45, (pictured) who was found with what is believed to be a self-inflicted stab wound to his neck, is not being treated as suspicious.

A Home Office post-mortem examination carried out earlier today, Wednesday 24 January, found the cause of death for both girls was knife wounds to the neck.

Previous Home Office post-mortem examinations were carried out on Sunday 21 January and established the woman died as a result of stab wounds to the neck while the man died as a result of a stab wound to the neck.

Following the cause of deaths being established, detectives have confirmed that the deaths of Kanticha, Jasmin and Natasha are being treated as murder.

The death of Bartlomiej is not being treated as suspicious and officers are not looking for anyone else in connection with the deaths. 

However, detectives will continue with their enquiries for the coronial process.

Detective Chief Inspector Chris Burgess, who is leading the investigation, said: 'We understand how incredibly shocking this incident is for all involved and for the wider community and that this latest update adds to the sense of heartbreak. 

'Our thoughts are with the family of the those found at the property grieving such a traumatic loss.

'We would like to thank the community for their help and will continue to provide reassurance patrols in the area. 

'A cordon will remain on at the address whilst we finalise our detailed examination of the scene.'

The incident has been referred to the Independent Office of Police Conduct (IOPC) because of Kuczynski's prior phone call, and police contact at the address on December 14 after he was reported missing.

The IOPC's Charmaine Arbouin said earlier this week: 'Norfolk Constabulary established that the man had made a 999 call to the force shortly before 6am on January 19.

'During that call, the man expressed concerns for his own mental state saying he was confused. He was advised to seek medical advice and police did not attend.

Bartlomiej Kuczynski was found dead at home with his two young daughters and sister-in-law, not pictured here, at a property in Costessey, near Norwich

Flowers left at the scene in Costessey where a man, woman and two girls were found dead last week. Detectives are treating the deaths of the woman and the two girls as murder

The IOPC is also investigating contact the force had with Mr Kuczynski (pictured with one of his children) on December 14 after he was reported missing

'At around 7am the force received a call from a dog walker concerned for the welfare of those within the home. Officers attended shortly after and, tragically, found the man, a woman and two children dead.

'Following a mandatory referral from the force we have decided to investigate and will be examining if the force's handling of the contact they had with the man was appropriate and in line with force policy, training and procedures.

'We will be making contact with the families of the deceased to explain our role. Our sympathies are with the families and friends of the deceased and all those affected by this incident.'

Norfolk Police received the 999 call from Mr Kuczynski at around 6am on Friday but 'resources were not deployed'.

They finally responded after a second call around an hour later from a member of the public who 'wanted to raise a concern about the people inside'.

Police at the scene in Costessey, Norfolk, after the four bodies were found inside a house on January 19

Post-mortem examinations have determined that Kanticha Sukpengpanao, 36, Jasmin Kuczynska, 12 and Natasha Kuczynska, eight, died of stab wounds to the neck (pictured: forensics officers on the scene)

Officers arrived at 7.15am and broke in to find the bodies of Polish-born Mr Kuczynski, 45, his daughters, and their aunt, Ms Sukpengpanao, of Bangkok, Thailand. 

The girls' mother, Nanthaka Kuczynska, was at her job as a caretaker at the time. 

Ms Sukpengpanao's best friend Naty Wathanakul has confirmed Mr Kuczynski had a 'mental health issue' and that he had given up his job. 

She added: 'We don't know the detail.'

Mrs Kuczynska has been left 'shocked and heartbroken' by the tragedy, said Ms Wathanakul, who speculated that 'petite' and 'beautiful' Ms Sukpengpanao was 'in the wrong place at the wrong time' and may have died fighting to 'protect her nieces'.

Ms Wathanakul went on to say of Ms Sukpengpanao: 'She loved her nieces, I always saw their pictures together. She is a single woman, no boyfriend, no husband, that's why she spent most of her time with those lovely girls.'

Neighbours have also said Mr Kuczynski had been suffering with mental health problems for months and had asked for help, although they didn't know if he had engaged with mental health services. 

He was reported missing on December 14, prompting officers to search woodland before finding him on the Queen's Hills estate, after which he was taken to Norfolk and Norwich University Hospital (NNUH) for an assessment.

However, he left the site unescorted before it was carried out. Norfolk Police is currently involved in the roll-out of a scheme called Right Care, Right Person, which aims to reduce the amount of time police spend attending mental health calls. 

An NNUH spokesperson said: 'We can confirm that the patient was taken to the emergency department by police on December 14.

'Following a comprehensive clinical assessment, the patient was assessed as having mental capacity and he left the unit before being reviewed again by the team.'

The hospital trust itself does not provide mental health services, with this responsibility lying with the Norfolk and Suffolk NHS Foundation Trust, but its A&E facilities can be used by NSFT for urgent mental health assessments.

A forensics tent was erected outside a house on the new-build Queen's Hills housing estate on the edge of Costessey

The Trust has so far declined to comment, citing the ongoing police investigation; the force itself continues to face questions over its earlier responses to incidents involving Mr Kuczynski. 

Norfolk's Chief Constable Paul Sanford said in a statement on Monday: 'It's only right that when there are questions about our response, they are properly investigated, and I fully support and welcome this scrutiny.

'I will not wait for the outcome of this investigation to review our working practices and that process has started.

'Today, I have asked the Inspectorate to consider undertaking a deeper review than the robust inspection already planned, to provide the reassurance that the public and I need in our response to emergency calls.'

The county's police and crime commissioner, Giles Orpen-Smellie, has stated he will 'robustly' hold the chief constable to account for the actions of officers and other staff.

Floral tributes have been left outside the house in Costessey, while pupils at the schools the girls attended have been offered support.

Dr Roger Harris, the headteacher at 1,200-pupil Taverham High School where Jasmin was a pupil, said: 'She was a really lovely, quiet girl who was always smiling and a little bit cheeky at times. As a parent, you would be proud to call her your daughter.

'I found out what had happened early Friday afternoon and I was devastated. I didn't believe it and frankly, didn't want to believe it. I shared the information with staff that that day before notifying parents of pupils at the school that evening.'

Dr Harris and Matthew Cross, headteacher at Queen's Hill Primary where Natasha was a pupil, attended Queen's Hills Community Centre with the county council's Critical Incident Team to offer children an opportunity to share their feelings and provide extra support on Sunday.

Support continues to be offered to children in the school this week, and a book of condolence has been set up for pupils to sign.

Dr Harris said the school would also be talking to pupils to organise a permanent memorial for Jasmin.

Mr Cross said: 'We're devastated by the loss of Natasha. Our thoughts continue to be with her family and all those affected by this tragic incident. Natasha was the kind of child that any teacher would want in their class.

'She arrived every day with a smile on her face and was committed to all aspects of school life. Natasha enjoyed sharing her love of reading with many of our younger pupils and was always there to help others across the school.

'She approached her learning with pride and curiosity while enjoying coming to school, spending time with her friends and making them laugh. Her friends, our staff and all of our school community will miss her.'
