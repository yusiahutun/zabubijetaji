Stop Body Shaming Politicians - Fat Shaming and Donald Trump

March 02, 2024
blog

It was the year I finally confessed to my doctor that my eating disorder had made my life unmanageable. It was also the year that Donald Trump got elected.

These things should have been unconnected, but that summer a photo of Chris Christie sitting on a beach was published as part of a news story. The commentary went something like this:

He was slow roasting those ham hocks he calls legs.He needs to buy a Homer Simpson mumu or something. I’m tired of seeing his gunt.Get a trebuchet or a forklift or a fleet of helicopters or whatever and remove his corpulence from office!(Yes, I know I’m fat-shaming. No, I don’t care, because f*ck him.)

The political offense Christie had committed (bathing on a beach otherwise closed to the public on a hot summer day) was overshadowed by the crime people were most aggrieved about: being a fat person on a beach.

"The political offense Christie had committed was overshadowed by the crime people were most aggrieved about: being a fat person on a beach."

This was not something I found on the belligerent part of the internet—it wasn’t a troll site of incendiary articles meant to further radicalize Twitter eggs and racist uncles. This was a liberal website. I imagined the writers and commenters were people like my friends and loved ones. This is what made it hurt so much.

Courtesy of Chrissy Stockton

"I imagined the writers and commenters were people like my friends and loved ones. This is what made it hurt so much," writes Chrissy Stockton, pictured on a hike in Upstate New York

A few months earlier I’d started the long journey of recovery from binge eating disorder. I found a therapist and a dietician and a group; I spent hours every week talking about food and body issues. I learned that for me, the cessation of my eating disorder depended on the cessation of the thing that caused it: shame. Specifically, shame I felt about the way I looked which motivated each descent into the binge-restrict cycle.

The way to get rid of shame is to pull it out of the dark and talk about it, which is exactly the opposite thing you feel like doing when you experience shame. It’s a distressing enough process that my subconscious originally thought an eating disorder would be preferable—the option to distract and numb seems a much surer (and more pleasant) means of survival than having to wade slowly through the pain.

Courtesy of Chrissy Stockton

"Body shaming is now part of our media diet as we engage in debates and collective fascination over how much the president weighs," writes the author

A woman I met in one of my groups summed it up. She was my mother’s age and had a good marriage and grown children who loved her. She was healthy after surviving a battle with cancer. Each of these things ticked off my biggest insecurities, the things that made me think “If I just had what she has I would be happy with myself.” But nearly 90 percent of women feel some sense of shame about their body, so it wasn’t too surprising to learn that even a loving husband and adoring children and a clean bill of health can’t insulate you.

“I feel like I’m a monster,” she said, “When I look at my body, I feel like I am looking at a monster’s body. I don’t know how someone could love me.”

I recognized this as the drum beating under the surface of my skin. I’d never said the m-word out loud when I talked about my food and body issues, but it was there. I thought I was a monster too.

It actually sounds a little ridiculous to say out loud. As much as I empathize with the woman in my group, I also recognized that her vision was obstructed by her eating disorder. No sane person would think she was unlovable. And yet, when I tried to fact-check whether my own thoughts were rational, I thought of the ways my internal dialogue sounded just like the commentary surrounding the Chris Christie story.

People think my thighs look like ham hocks.

People hate looking at me and wish I’d hide my body so they wouldn’t have to see it.

People are disgusted by my body, they just won’t say it because I’m nice to them.

It’s not just the beach story. Body shaming is now part of our media diet as we read articles about how we miss Obama because he wasn’t fat, gleefully laugh as naked statues of Trump (complete with micro-penis) appear in public, and engage in debates and collective fascination over how much the president weighs.

"To be a fat person in the midst of all of this, trying to convince myself that I am worthy while existing in this body, is a fight I lose."

To be a fat person in the midst of all of this, trying to convince myself that I am worthy and lovable while existing in this body, is a fight I honestly lose a lot of days.

I am working on my recovery and I am trying to take responsibility for the way I feel. I don’t think anyone owes me a safe space to live in, but I do think people who engage in body shaming have mistaken beliefs about what it is they are doing.

There is no “good” or “effective” kind of shame, says shame researcher Brené Brown in her book I Thought It Was Just Me (But It Isn't): Making the Journey From "What Will People Think?" to "I Am Enough." Using shame because we don’t agree with someone’s decision-making or beliefs, like the ideologies of our president, won’t effect lasting change. We also probably won’t harm the intended target, because their narcissism or celebrity makes the insult unlikely to reach them or be absorbed. But we will hurt plenty of other people. Our friends and loved ones are listening to what we say (along with strangers we might not care directly about, but definitely don’t intend to harm) and taking in information about their own worthiness from how we judge the bodies of others.

"I'm not asking you to be kind to a politician’s body because the politician deserves it, but rather, because everyone else does."

I'm not asking people to be kind to a politician’s body because the politician deserves it, but rather, because everyone else does. It’s about having awareness of who is reading the words and what the story says about what we, as a culture, find acceptable or unacceptable. When someone talks about how someone’s body is unacceptable it doesn’t happen in a vacuum and intentionally or not you’re going to shame anyone listening who is going to compare their own body to the intended target’s.

I’m still here. I’m still healing. I am trying every day to put one foot in front of the other and get to a place where wondering about what other people think of me doesn’t make a panic rise in my belly. I think I will get better, but I can also request that we do better. I have a front row seat, I know firsthand all this shaming isn’t helping anyone.

Chrissy Stockton is a partner and creative at Thought Catalog.
