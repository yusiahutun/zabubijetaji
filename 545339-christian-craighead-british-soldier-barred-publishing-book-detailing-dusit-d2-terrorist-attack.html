Christian Craighead: British Soldier Barred from Publishing Book Detailing Dusit D2 Terrorist Attack

April 24, 2024

  • UK soldier Christian Craighead was in Kenya during the Dusit D2 terrorist attack and was among those who led the rescue operation
  • Craighead charged into the building, neutralised several terrorists and saved countless lives in the process
  • The special forces soldier has since written a book titled One Man In, which details his experiences on that fateful day

A British soldier who goes by the pseudonym Christian Craighead has been barred from publishing his book on the 2019 Dusit D2 attack that claimed 21 lives.

In the book, Craighead highlights his courageous actions during the crisis, where he played a significant role in saving lives and confronting terrorists.

The book titled One Man In: The Explosive Firsthand Account of the Lone Special-Ops Soldier Who Fought Off a Major Terrorist Attack in Kenya was supposed to have been published in 2023.

This prohibition stems from an October 2023 ruling by British Justice Steyn, who cited regulations that prevent military personnel from releasing information that could potentially endanger national security.

"He has only been prevented from giving an account of an operation, the Dusit incident, in which he engaged as a member of UK special forces,” Justice Steyn said during a private hearing.

According to the Telegraph, the judge also considered the implications for all parties connected to the Dusit incident, including the victims and the UK Special Air Service (SAS).

“Those community interests entail the protection of lives, the protection of national security, the maintenance of the morale and efficiency of UKSF and protecting relations with defence partners."“On the evidence before me, the interests of the community substantially outweigh the claimant's interest in publishing a memoir about the Dusit incident (even when buttressed by the interest of the public in receiving the information he wishes to disclose, and by the interest of his publisher)," Steyn added.

Craighead had hoped to challenge the decision but was ultimately denied the opportunity to appeal.
